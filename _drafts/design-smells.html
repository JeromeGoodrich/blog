So this presentation is about design smells. you know RFIVNNO...

But before we get into each smell. I wanted to take some time to actually explore the significance of this paradigm and why it might be worth your time to learn.

We describe design smells viscerally, like a 6th sense. our design nose tells us when our code is not easy to change. Unlike defects in the code, which might fail a test design is not something immediately apparent, much harder to pin down and yet vital, because as we will see the quicker we are to pick up on a design smell the better off we are.

Why? Well I like to think of these smells as negative rules of OO design. Having a design nose and being able to identify these smells is equivalent to an understanding of OO Design.

Why do we even talk about design smells? Design Smells are ostensibly problems in our codebase and I like to think of them as the t=0 of Agile Development. Because agile operates under the assumption that things will go wrong. Everything we do in agile is for the purpose of preventing design smells via Agile practices like planning, pairing, refactoring, etc. or for getting rid of a smell when it comes up via principles and patterns. As such, I'd argue it's important to understand design smells, in order to understand and be effective in agile

What are design smells? So I mentioned that they are problems in our code base, but what does that even mean? I'd like to propose that design smells are not only problems in the code base, but a quality of it. What I mean by this, is that design smells point to our code exhibiting certain undesirable characteristics, the code could work, all the test could pass, but there is still something wrong. It's like a person on the verge of a cold, they might be functioning now, but give it a day or two and they'll be out of commission. There's a reason why they are called design smells. Because they deal with the overall design of a program, not necessarily its function though the two are often inexorably linked.

If the ideal of code is for it to be easily changed, then design smells are symptoms of this not being the case. Agile development is all about building code that conforms to this ideal, and therefore it is all about dealing with things that take the code further away from the ideal. There is no agile without an understanding of design smells, which we can otherwise call different ways that makes software difficult to change. When code exhibits a design smell, it doesn't necessarily mean that it's broken, but it does mean that when it does break things will often be much more difficult to fix.
